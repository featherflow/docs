## Why Infrastructure Comes Before Everything Else

Before you write a single line of copy, three things need to be true about your sending domain:

1. **SPF** is configured correctly
2. **DKIM** is configured correctly
3. **DMARC** is configured correctly

If any one of these is missing or wrong, major email providers will filter your messages before they reach the inbox. No amount of great copy fixes a spam score problem.

---

## The Three Auth Records Explained

### SPF (Sender Policy Framework)

Tells receiving servers which IP addresses are authorised to send email on behalf of your domain.

**What it prevents:** Someone else spoofing your domain in the `From:` field.

**What it looks like in DNS:**
```
Type: TXT
Name: @
Value: v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all
```

Replace `_spf.google.com` with your mail provider's SPF record. Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, and every major sending platform publishes this value in their docs.

### DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)

Adds a cryptographic signature to every outbound email. Receiving servers use the public key in your DNS to verify the signature — proving the email hasn't been tampered with in transit.

**What it prevents:** Email content being modified after sending (and signals trustworthiness to inbox providers).

**How to set it up:** Your email platform generates a unique DKIM key pair. You add the public key as a TXT record in DNS. In Google Workspace: Admin → Apps → Google Workspace → Gmail → Authenticate email. Copy the TXT record value and add it to your DNS.

### DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance)

Tells receiving servers what to do when an email fails SPF or DKIM checks — and sends you reports about authentication failures.

**Start with monitoring mode (p=none) before enforcing:**
```
Type: TXT
Name: _dmarc
Value: v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourdomain.com
```

Once you've reviewed a few weeks of reports and confirmed legitimate mail is passing, move to `p=quarantine` and eventually `p=reject`.

<Warning>
  **DNS propagation takes time.** Changes take 15 minutes to 48 hours to propagate globally. Run your verification checks after your DNS TTL expires — not immediately after saving the records.
</Warning>

## Domain Warmup: Building Your Sender Reputation

A brand-new domain that suddenly sends 200 emails per day looks like a spammer. Email providers build a reputation score for every domain over time — you need to ramp gradually.

| Week | Sends/Day | What to Watch | Red Flag |
|------|-----------|--------------|---------|
| 1–2 | 5–15 | Bounce rate, spam complaints | Bounce > 5% = stop and fix |
| 3–4 | 20–40 | Open rate, reply rate | Open rate < 25% = review targeting |
| 5–6 | 50–80 | Inbox placement | Use GlockApps to check placement |
| 7+ | 100–150 | Sustained deliverability | Monitor weekly, never stop caring |

**Use a warmup tool for the first 6 weeks.** Warmup Inbox, Lemwarm, and Mailreach all send and receive emails automatically to build your reputation with real inbox providers. This runs in the background — you don't need to manage it manually.

<Tip>
  **Pro tip:** Use a subdomain for outbound prospecting (`outreach.yourdomain.com`), not your primary domain. This isolates your prospecting reputation from your transactional email reputation. If your outbound domain gets flagged, your product emails are unaffected.
</Tip>

## The Deliverability Checklist

Before your first send:

- [ ] SPF record added and verified (MXToolbox shows green)
- [ ] DKIM configured and verified
- [ ] DMARC added in `p=none` monitoring mode
- [ ] Warmup tool connected and running
- [ ] Sending from a subdomain (not primary domain)
- [ ] Unsubscribe link or mechanism in place
- [ ] Sending volume under 20/day for first two weeks
